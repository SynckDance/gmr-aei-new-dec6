Introduction

What is Global Movement Research?

Dances are not simply performed—they are inherited, carried, and sometimes lost. GMR is a long-term research project that traces how movement traditions emerge from specific places and travel across villages, cities, oceans, and digital media.

Most dance archives are frozen—collections of video and notation stored for preservation but rarely touched. They hold the shape of a body's gesture but cannot respond to questions about where that gesture came from, who else moved that way, or why it survived when so much else did not.

GMR experiments with unfreezing these archives. We are building methods to transform them from static storage into what we call motion archives, then living archives, and ultimately thinking archives—systems that can discover relationships between dances, trace patterns of diffusion, and support research that was previously impossible.

Stakes

Why does this matter?

Dance traditions carry cultural memory in ways that text and image cannot fully capture. When communities are displaced—by colonialism, migration, or environmental change—their dances often travel with them, adapting and persisting in diaspora.

But these histories are difficult to trace. A gesture performed in Salvador da Bahia might echo one performed in the Niger Delta centuries ago, yet no archive can currently detect that connection automatically. Traditional cataloging misses what the body remembers.

We need archives that can do more than store. We need archives that can think—that can help researchers, communities, and practitioners discover the relationships between movements across time and space. Not to replace human expertise, but to augment it.

Framework

From Frozen to Thinking

GMR proposes a continuum for understanding what archives can become:

Frozen Archive

Storage without action. Videos and documents sit in repositories, accessible but inert. Most dance archives remain here.

Motion Archive

Movement data extracted and structured. Bodies become searchable points in space. The archive begins to speak in coordinates.

Living Archive

Relationships emerge. Dances are compared, clustered, and connected. The archive grows as new material arrives.

Thinking Archive

The archive asks questions. It proposes hypotheses about movement diffusion, flags anomalies, and supports research that humans alone could not conduct.
