Global Movement Research

How dances move, survive disruption, and how archives can begin to learn with the bodies they hold.

Global Movement Research (GMR) is a long term project that combines fieldwork, motion capture, geospatial thinking, and AI supported analysis to study how movement traditions emerge, travel, and change.

What is Global Movement Research

Dances are not simply performances. They are practices that people learn, carry, adapt, and sometimes lose. Each movement tradition grows in conversation with particular places, histories, and communities.

Global Movement Research investigates how these traditions take shape in specific regions and how they later appear in new locations, including diasporic and digital contexts. The project combines:

  • embodied knowledge from dancers and communities
  • archival and historical research
  • motion capture and other movement recording tools
  • computational methods that can compare and visualize large amounts of movement data

The goal is not to replace human interpretation. GMR builds technical tools that support dancers, scholars, and communities in asking questions about movement that are difficult to address with traditional archives alone.

Why this matters

Movement traditions carry forms of memory that are often underrepresented in written or photographic archives. They can reflect experiences of work, ritual, celebration, discipline, joy, grief, and resistance. When people move across borders because of trade, education, conflict, colonialism, or migration, they often re-create and transform these dances in new settings.

Tracing these histories is difficult. The same kinesthetic idea can surface in distant regions and at different points in time. Conventional archival systems usually organize material by title, date, and location, but they rarely store the movement in a way that allows researchers to compare structure, phrasing, or kinesthetic feel across many examples.

GMR argues that we need archives that can support this kind of inquiry in a transparent and accountable way. The project tests methods that treat movement data as one entry point among many, alongside testimony, historical records, and community knowledge.

How this site fits into the project

GMR operates across several connected environments:

  • Communities and studios where dancers teach, learn, revise, and record practices in context.
  • Research labs where motion capture, Qualitative Trajectory Calculus (QTC), sequence alignment methods (SAMs), and other tools are developed and tested.
  • The GMR StoryMap which presents selected regions, confluence zones, and case studies in a narrative, map based format.
  • This site which introduces the project, shares sample materials, and documents the methods that sit behind the work.

The StoryMap focuses on geographies, routes, and case studies. It is the best place to follow specific regions, such as river systems, coastal corridors, or urban clusters, and to see how GMR thinks about confluences.

This site focuses on the overall research architecture. It shows how dances move through the Dance Replication Process, how archives shift from frozen to motion and living states, and how Artificial Embodied Intelligence (AEI) is used to activate existing movement records under clear ethical constraints.

From Frozen to Thinking

GMR treats archives as systems that can change over time, moving through several states.

Frozen Archive

Storage focused collections. Video, notation, and documents are preserved and indexed. Material can be accessed, but the archive does not analyze relationships within the movement itself.

Motion Archive

Movement data is extracted and structured, for example as skeletal coordinates or QTC sequences. Researchers can pose basic questions in terms of motion, such as repeated pathways, directions, or use of space.

Living Archive

Recordings are compared, clustered, and linked. Computational tools highlight patterns and potential relationships between dances. The archive grows as new material and new analyses are added.

Thinking Archive

The archive supports hypothesis generation. It can suggest possible connections, highlight unusual cases, and propose diffusion patterns that merit further study. Any output remains subject to human review, community feedback, and historical context.

What you can do on this site

Explore Confluences

Navigate selected research sites where movement traditions intersect or interact. Each confluence highlights a place, associated dances, and questions that guide ongoing research.

Watch 3D Reconstructions

View skeletal reconstructions generated from motion capture sessions. These clips function as research artifacts that help reveal timing, pathways, and structural features of the recorded dances.

Compare Dances

Place two dances in dialogue using side by side visualizations and similarity measures. Explore where their structures align or differ and use those findings as prompts for further embodied and historical inquiry.

Read the Methods

Learn how the project handles motion data, from the Dance Replication Process to the Atomic Confluences Framework, QTC and SAMs, and the AEI Activation Framework. See how these pieces fit together inside the archive.
