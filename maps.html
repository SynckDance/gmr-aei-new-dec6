Atomic Confluences

Global Movement Research uses Atomic Confluences to describe regions where dance, migration, trade, and cultural memory cluster into dense movement ecologies. Each confluence is treated as a regional node made up of multiple city-states, river systems, and border zones, rather than a single city alone.

Visualization of global dance diffusion patterns and research sites

The Atomic Confluences Framework

Confluences are identified using a set of interconnected criteria: geographical proximity, coordinates, language, religious affiliations, shared cultural histories, agricultural and natural resources, population, city-state structures, musical rhythms, dance styles, climate, resettlement information, geopolitical relations, and classification precision.

These attributes work together to define regions where movement traditions have historically clustered, traveled, and transformed. The framework allows researchers to trace how dances move across boundaries while respecting the complexity of cultural exchange.

West Africa

Two active confluences with motion capture data from Nigeria and Ghana.

WA#6

Eko Confluence

Nigeria: Edo, Bini, and Tiv Peoples

Region and Focus

The Eko Confluence centers on the southern and central regions of present-day Nigeria, encompassing the historical territories of the Edo (Benin Kingdom), Bini, and Tiv peoples. "Eko" references the Yoruba name for Lagos, acknowledging the region's role as a major coastal confluence point where inland and Atlantic trade routes have historically intersected. The confluence extends from the Niger Delta through the Benue River valley, capturing movement traditions that reflect both forest and savanna cultural zones.

Migrational Diffusions

Key nodes within this confluence include Benin City, the Benue River corridor, the Cross River region, and connections northward toward the Jos Plateau. These pathways represent centuries of trade, religious practice, and cultural exchange between coastal and inland communities.

Dance Research Status

Eight dances are under focused investigation in this confluence. Motion capture data is available and has been processed into skeletal reconstructions for QTC analysis and visualization.

8 dances with mocap data
Bata Bata Extended Ekombi Esapkaide I Esapkaide II Esapkaide III Swange Ugho

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

The Eko region satisfies key confluence criteria through its position at the intersection of multiple ethnic territories, its long history as a trade and cultural center, and the persistence of distinct dance traditions that carry historical memory. The Edo kingdom's documented artistic traditions, the Tiv people's distinctive movement vocabulary, and the region's connections to both Atlantic and trans-Saharan networks make it a dense node for studying how dances travel and transform.

WA#3

Ashanti Confluence

Ghana: Ewe and Ga Peoples

Region and Focus

The Ashanti Confluence covers a multi-country region centered on present-day Ghana but extending into Togo, Côte d'Ivoire, Burkina Faso, and Niger. It uses the historical Ashanti Kingdom as a naming anchor while emphasizing the region's long history as a trading and cultural meeting point, including coastal access to the Gulf of Guinea. The current research focuses specifically on dances from the Ewe and Ga peoples of southeastern Ghana.

Migrational Diffusions

Migration and cultural diffusion have been identified in key areas including Tamale, Tumu, Garu, Ho, Abidjan, Tarkwa, Accra, Lomé, Bassar, and Kumasi, each functioning as a local constellation within the larger confluence.

Dance Research Status

Seven dances are under focused investigation in this confluence, representing Ewe and Ga traditions. Motion capture data is available and has been processed into skeletal reconstructions.

7 dances with mocap data
Adzogbo Agbadza Atsiagbekor Bata Djole Fumefume Kpanlongo

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

Ashanti satisfies nearly all confluence criteria: dense trade history, strong archival and academic presence (the University of Ghana at Legon is a prominent center for dance education in the region), active performance traditions, and a long record of coastal and inland exchange. The region scores highly across categories including dance styles, musical rhythms, shared cultural histories, language, agricultural activities, and city-state structure.

Caribbean

Diasporic confluence tracing movement traditions from West Africa through the Atlantic.

CB#1

Havana Confluence

Cuba: Orisha Traditions

Region and Focus

The Havana Confluence represents a major diasporic node where West African movement traditions, particularly those of the Yoruba, were carried across the Atlantic and transformed in the context of colonial Cuba. The Orisha religious tradition preserved and adapted dances associated with specific deities, creating a living archive of movement that maintains connections to its West African origins while developing distinctly Caribbean characteristics.

Diasporic Connections

This confluence connects directly to the Eko and Ashanti confluences in West Africa, representing the Atlantic dimension of dance diffusion. Research traces how movement vocabularies associated with Yoruba religious practice traveled through the Middle Passage and were reconstituted in Cuban contexts, particularly in Havana, Matanzas, and Santiago de Cuba.

Dance Research Status

Three Orisha dances have been captured and processed, each associated with specific deities in the Lucumí tradition. These recordings allow for direct comparison with West African source traditions.

3 dances with mocap data
Changó Kuele Kuele Yemayá

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

Havana functions as a confluence because it concentrates multiple streams of African diasporic movement practice in a single urban zone with documented historical depth. The survival of Orisha dance traditions provides a unique opportunity to study how movement vocabularies persist and transform across forced migration, making this confluence essential for understanding the Atlantic dimension of global movement research.

South Asia

Classical and folk dance traditions from the island of Sri Lanka.

SA#1

Kandy Confluence

Sri Lanka: Kandyan Tradition

Region and Focus

The Kandy Confluence centers on the central highlands of Sri Lanka, where the Kandyan Kingdom maintained its independence until the early 19th century and developed a distinctive court dance tradition. The city of Kandy, with its Temple of the Tooth and associated ritual practices, serves as the primary node, but the confluence extends throughout the central and southern regions where related folk and ritual dance forms persist.

Historical Connections

Sri Lanka's position on Indian Ocean trade routes connects this confluence to broader networks of cultural exchange spanning South Asia, Southeast Asia, and the East African coast. The Kandyan tradition itself synthesizes indigenous practices with influences absorbed through centuries of trade and religious contact.

Dance Research Status

Six dances representing court, ritual, and folk traditions have been captured. The corpus includes the distinctive Vannama tradition, ritual dances associated with the Kohomba Kankariya ceremony, and regional folk forms.

6 dances with mocap data
Baila Kappirinna Folk & Court Dance Gajaga Vannama (Fast) Gajaga Vannama (Slow) Kohomba Kankariya Telme Ruhunu

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

Kandy qualifies as an atomic confluence through its documented historical continuity, the institutional preservation of dance knowledge through temple and court systems, and its position within broader Indian Ocean exchange networks. The survival of multiple dance genres (ritual, court, folk) within a relatively compact geographic zone makes it ideal for studying how movement traditions differentiate and persist within a single cultural matrix.

Europe

Vernacular dance traditions from Ireland.

EU#1

Irish Confluence

Ireland: Step Dance Traditions

Region and Focus

The Irish Confluence encompasses the island of Ireland, with particular attention to regional variations in step dance that developed across different counties and communities. Irish step dance represents a vernacular tradition that has been both preserved through competitive structures and transformed through globalization, making it an important case study for how dance traditions institutionalize and travel.

Diasporic Dimensions

Irish dance has significant diasporic extensions, particularly in North America and Australia, where immigrant communities maintained and adapted traditions. These global connections make the Irish confluence relevant for comparative study with other diasporic movement traditions in the GMR archive.

Dance Research Status

Five step dances representing different forms within the tradition have been captured, including jigs, reels, and the distinctive sean-nós (old style) form that predates competitive standardization.

5 dances with mocap data
Festival Blackbird Garden of Daisies Molyneaux Blackbird Sean-nós Jigs Slip Jig

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

Ireland functions as a confluence through the concentration of distinct movement traditions within a bounded geographic and cultural zone, combined with well-documented historical development and extensive diaspora. The contrast between competitive and vernacular forms, and between regional styles, provides material for studying how traditions differentiate, institutionalize, and travel.

Americas

Dance traditions from Brazil and Puerto Rico.

AM#1

Recife Confluence

Brazil: Pernambuco Region

Region and Focus

The Recife Confluence centers on the northeastern Brazilian state of Pernambuco, particularly the cities of Recife and Olinda, where Carnival traditions have developed distinctive dance forms. This region represents another node in the Atlantic diasporic network, with documented historical connections to both West Africa and Portugal.

Dance Research Status

One dance form from this region has been captured: Frevo, the acrobatic Carnival dance characteristic of Pernambuco. This initial capture establishes the confluence as an active research site with potential for expanded documentation.

1 dance with mocap data
Frevo

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

Recife qualifies as a confluence through its position as a major Atlantic port with documented African diasporic influence, the distinctive development of regional dance forms, and connections to broader Brazilian and Latin American movement networks. The region's Carnival traditions demonstrate how dance functions as cultural memory and community practice.

AM#2

San Juan Confluence

Puerto Rico: Latin Social Dance

Region and Focus

The San Juan Confluence represents Puerto Rico's position as a node in Caribbean and Latin American social dance networks. The island's location and history have made it a site where African, European, and indigenous influences combined to produce distinctive dance forms that subsequently traveled throughout the Americas.

Dance Research Status

One social dance form has been captured, representing the contemporary Latin social dance tradition. This initial capture positions Puerto Rico as an emerging research site within the GMR framework.

1 dance with mocap data
Salsa

Why It Is an Atomic Confluence

Puerto Rico functions as a confluence through its historical role in the development and dissemination of Latin social dance forms, its connections to both Caribbean and mainland American dance networks, and the continuing vitality of participatory dance culture. The island's position allows for comparative study with other Caribbean confluences.
