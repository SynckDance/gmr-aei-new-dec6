Technical Framework

Methods

GMR is not just a collection of dances. It is an experiment in methodology—an attempt to build tools that can help researchers ask questions about movement that were previously impossible to answer at scale.

This page offers a brief introduction to the core technical concepts that underpin the project. For full details, see the linked documentation.

Framework

Frozen, Motion, Living, Thinking Archives

Most dance archives are frozen. They hold video, notation, and documentation, but they cannot respond to queries, discover relationships, or generate new knowledge. They are storage systems, not thinking systems.

GMR proposes a continuum of archive states, each building on the last:

Frozen Archive

Traditional storage. Files sit in repositories, organized by metadata, accessible but inert. The archive cannot answer questions it was not explicitly designed for.

Motion Archive

Movement data is extracted from video or motion capture and structured as searchable coordinates. The archive can now be queried spatially: "Show me all dances where the arms rise above shoulder height."

Living Archive

Dances are compared, clustered, and connected. Relationships emerge between movements that were never explicitly linked. The archive grows and adapts as new material arrives.

Thinking Archive

The archive proposes hypotheses. It flags anomalies, suggests possible diffusion pathways, and supports research that humans alone could not conduct. It thinks with the bodies it holds.

Process

Nine-Step Dance Replication Process (DRP)

DRP is GMR's workflow for moving dances from discovery to archive. It is not a linear pipeline but a cyclical process with ethical checkpoints at every stage.

The nine steps are:

  1. Discovery — Identifying dances for study, often through community partnership or archival research.
  2. Documentation — Recording movement through video, motion capture, and ethnographic notes.
  3. Contextualization — Gathering historical, social, and cultural information about the dance and its practitioners.
  4. Extraction — Converting video or mocap data into structured movement representations (e.g., skeletal coordinates, QTC sequences).
  5. Analysis — Applying computational methods (SAMs, clustering) to discover patterns and relationships.
  6. Interpretation — Working with domain experts and community members to make sense of computational findings.
  7. Validation — Testing interpretations against additional evidence and community knowledge.
  8. Archiving — Storing movement data, metadata, and interpretive notes in formats that support future research.
  9. Dissemination — Sharing findings with academic, practitioner, and source communities in appropriate forms.

Ethics is not a separate step but a thread running through all nine. At each stage, we ask: Who benefits? Who might be harmed? What permissions do we have? What obligations do we carry?

Computational Methods

QTC and SAMs

Qualitative Trajectory Calculus (QTC)

QTC is a formalism for describing how objects move in relation to each other. Originally developed for robotics, we adapt it to describe how body parts move relative to each other and to space.

Instead of tracking exact coordinates, QTC captures relationships: Are the hands approaching each other or moving apart? Is the torso rotating toward or away from the direction of travel? Is the movement accelerating or decelerating?

This relational representation is robust to differences in body size, capture conditions, and performance style. Two dancers of different heights performing the "same" movement will produce similar QTC signatures even if their absolute coordinates differ.

Sequence Alignment Methods (SAMs)

SAMs are algorithms for comparing sequences—originally developed for DNA analysis, now applied to dance. Given two QTC sequences representing two dances, SAMs can identify regions of similarity and difference.

The key insight is that dances, like genes, can be thought of as sequences of discrete elements. Alignment algorithms can detect shared "motifs"—movement phrases that appear in multiple traditions—even when they occur at different speeds or are embedded in different contexts.

High similarity scores do not prove historical connection, but they can flag candidates for further investigation. SAMs help us see patterns that would be invisible to the naked eye.

Activation Framework

AEI: Artificial Embodied Intelligence

AEI is GMR's framework for thinking about what AI can and cannot do with movement archives. The name is deliberate: we speak of "activation" rather than "generation" because the goal is not to create new dances but to bring archived movements into new forms of analysis and engagement.

AEI operates under several constraints:

Non-generative: AEI does not synthesize novel dances. It works with recorded movement, transforming representations (e.g., from video to skeleton to QTC) and discovering relationships, but it does not invent.

Ethically bounded: AEI incorporates permission structures. Movement data carries metadata about consent, attribution, and acceptable uses. The system respects these boundaries.

Interpretively humble: AEI proposes hypotheses; it does not declare truths. Computational findings are starting points for human interpretation, not endpoints.

Community-oriented: AEI is designed to support source communities, not replace them. Findings are shared back; communities have input into how their dances are represented and used.
