Methods

Technical Framework

Global Movement Research (GMR) is not just a collection of dances. It is an experiment in methodology, an attempt to build tools that let researchers ask questions about movement that were previously impossible to answer at scale.

This Methods section documents the core frameworks that shape the project:

  • the Dance Replication Process (DRP) for moving dances from discovery to archive
  • the Frozen, Motion, Living, and Thinking Archive continuum
  • the Atomic Confluences Framework (ACF) for geospatial diffusion
  • the Artificial Embodied Intelligence (AEI) Activation Framework
  • the use of Qualitative Trajectory Calculus (QTC) and Sequence Alignment Methods (SAMs) for comparative analysis across dances and regions

All of these are in active development. The site records the current state of the work and will be updated as new labs, collaborations, and datasets come online.

Process

Nine Step Dance Replication Process (DRP)

The Dance Replication Process (DRP) is GMR's workflow for moving dances from community to archive. It is a nine-step process that cycles between lab and lived practice. It includes feedback and ethics checks at every stage, rather than a simple one-way pipeline.

  1. Discovery Identifying and uncovering dances for study. Discovery can happen through community partnerships, personal networks, fieldwork, and archival research. At this stage, the focus is on recognizing a dance, a motif, or a practice that carries weight for a community and for the research questions at hand.
  2. Dispersal Understanding how the dance spreads across different communities and contexts. Dispersal asks: where else has this movement shown up, who is carrying it, and through what pathways—migration, ritual exchange, festivals, media, schooling. This step maps the circulation of the dance beyond a single site of origin.
  3. Analysis Using analytical methods such as QTC, SAMs, LMA, and related tools to examine the structure and logic of the dance. Here, movement is studied for its patterns, motifs, and internal rules. Analysis can include both qualitative and computational approaches, and often returns to Discovery and Dispersal as new questions emerge.
  4. Replication Performing and re-performing the dance in settings where it can be closely observed and recorded. Replication includes motion capture sessions, studio rehearsals, and guided demonstrations—always in dialogue with practitioners. The goal is not to replace community practice, but to create careful instances of the dance that can be studied, paused, and replayed.
  5. Data Converting recordings into structured motion data. This can include numerical trajectories, FBX files, CSV exports, QTC encodings, and detailed lab notes. At this stage, the dance becomes a set of linked data objects that can be processed, compared, and preserved while still being anchored to specific bodies and sessions.
  6. Interpretation Discussing and interpreting the data with dancers, culture bearers, and scholars. Interpretation asks what the patterns mean: emotionally, culturally, historically, and spiritually. Computational findings are treated as prompts that must be tested against lived knowledge, not as final answers.
  7. Contextualization Situating the dance within its geographical, historical, and cultural context, and within a wider vocabulary of related dances. Contextualization brings in political, economic, environmental, and diasporic forces that shape movement over time. It connects the data back to landscapes, lineages, and communities.
  8. Visualization Making the data accessible to audiences through visual formats. This includes films, diagrams, 3D models, StoryMaps, diffusion maps, motion capture replays, and interactive views of QTC or SAM results. Visualization is where the archive starts to become legible as more than a collection of files.
  9. Dissemination Sharing the visualized data and findings through performances, installations, websites, apps, StoryMaps, and publications. Dissemination also includes creating channels for people to deposit their own dance replications, respond to the findings, and shape future cycles of the process.

Across all nine steps, DRP keeps the body at the center. Dance is never only data. It remains a living practice that continues beyond the archive and beyond the lab.

Framework

Frozen, Motion, Living, Thinking Archives

Most dance archives are frozen. They hold video, notation, and documentation, but they cannot respond to queries, discover relationships, or generate new knowledge. They are storage systems, not thinking systems.

GMR proposes a continuum of archive states, each building on the last:

Frozen Archive

Traditional storage. Files sit in repositories, organized by metadata, accessible but inert. The archive cannot answer questions it was not explicitly catalogued for.

Motion Archive

Movement data is extracted from video or motion capture and structured as searchable coordinates. The archive can now be queried spatially—"show me all dances where arms rise above shoulder height" or "find sequences with circling footwork."

Living Archive

Dances are compared, clustered, and connected. Relationships emerge between movements that were never explicitly linked. The archive grows by learning from patterns in the data as well as from new contributions.

Thinking Archive

The archive proposes hypotheses. It flags anomalies, suggests possible diffusion pathways, and supports research that humans alone could not conduct at scale—while remaining accountable to source communities.

GMR's current prototypes sit between Motion and Living Archives. The AEI work described below is designed to move carefully toward the conditions of a Thinking Archive without abandoning ethical and community constraints.

Geospatial Diffusion Model

Atomic Confluences Framework (ACF)

The Atomic Confluences Framework is GMR's geolocation theory for understanding how dances begin, travel, and transform.

  • Atomic refers to a hypothesized center or cluster of origin—the smallest identifiable region where a pattern of movement first stabilizes in community practice.
  • Confluence refers to the flows that carry that movement beyond its origin: migration, trade, ritual exchange, schooling, media, tourism, war, religion, and other forms of contact.

In ACF, a dance is mapped as a field of confluences rather than a single point of authenticity. Bodies act as carriers of movement information, transporting and recombining motifs across borders and generations. Some atomic sites endure for centuries. Others disappear through displacement, violence, neglect, or erasure.

GMR uses ACF to:

  • place dances within regional confluence zones—for example, Niger Delta to Casamance coastal corridors
  • track how specific movement features appear, fade, recur, or recombine across locations
  • ask what political, economic, and environmental forces shape those flows and blockages

ACF connects close looking at the body to large-scale maps of movement, migration, and historical change.

Relational Motion Encoding

Qualitative Trajectory Calculus (QTC)

Qualitative Trajectory Calculus (QTC) is a framework for describing how objects move in relation to each other over time. Originally developed in spatial reasoning and robotics, GMR adapts QTC to capture how body parts move relative to each other and to space.

Instead of tracking exact numeric coordinates, QTC encodes relationships such as:

  • Are two joints approaching each other or moving apart?
  • Is one joint moving left or right, up or down, toward or away relative to another?
  • Is the relative motion accelerating, decelerating, or relatively stable?

These relationships are encoded as symbolic states—for example, negative, zero, or positive per dimension—forming a sequence of QTC "words" across time.

This qualitative representation is robust to differences in body size, capture conditions, and performance style. Two dancers of different heights performing the same movement may produce very similar QTC signatures even if their raw coordinates differ.

In GMR, QTC is the bridge that converts mocap CSVs into analyzable movement language. It allows comparison across bodies, recordings, and labs without demanding perfect numerical alignment.

Cross-Dance Comparison

Sequence Alignment Methods (SAMs)

Sequence Alignment Methods (SAMs) are algorithms for comparing sequences, originally developed for DNA and protein analysis and later applied to many kinds of temporal data. GMR applies SAMs to QTC sequences derived from dance.

Given two QTC sequences representing two dances or two performances, SAMs can:

  • identify regions of high similarity and difference
  • measure how strongly two dances share a recurring motif or phrase
  • detect shared movement patterns that appear in multiple traditions—even when they unfold at different speeds, scales, or in different contexts

The key insight is that dances, like genes, can be treated as sequences of discrete movement elements. Alignment algorithms can detect motifs that persist across geography and time and can flag candidates for further historical and ethnographic investigation.

High similarity scores do not prove direct historical connection. Instead, they function as hypothesis generators, pointing researchers toward patterns that might otherwise be invisible to the naked eye. Any claim about diffusion or influence must be tested through DRP, archival work, and community consultation—not through SAMs alone.

Activation Framework

AEI: Artificial Embodied Intelligence

Artificial Embodied Intelligence (AEI) is GMR's framework for thinking about what AI can and cannot do with movement archives. The emphasis is on activation, not generation. The goal is not to synthesize new dances from scratch, but to bring archived movements into new forms of analysis and engagement.

AEI operates under several constraints:

Non-generative

AEI does not invent new choreographies. It works with recorded movement, transforming representations—from video to skeleton to QTC—and discovering relationships within that data.

Ethically bounded

AEI incorporates permission structures. Movement data carries metadata about consent, attribution, and acceptable uses. The system is designed to respect these boundaries and to route sensitive material away from public models and high-risk applications.

Interpretively humble

AEI proposes hypotheses; it does not declare truths. Computational findings are starting points for human interpretation, not endpoints. Results are always brought back to practitioners, communities, and historical context for validation and critique.

Community-oriented

AEI is built to support source communities, not replace them. Findings are shared back. Communities have input into how their dances are represented, queried, and used—and they can contest or withdraw uses that do not align with their values.

In GMR, AEI is the engine that gradually moves the archive from motion to living and toward carefully thinking with, rather than over, the people whose movement built it.
